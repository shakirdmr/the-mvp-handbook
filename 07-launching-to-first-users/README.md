# Chapter 7 — Launching to Your First Users

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/07-hero.png)

The day you launch is the day most founders find out their product is real. Up until launch, your product is a thing in your head, your repo, and your test environment. The moment a stranger uses it, all the comfortable abstractions collapse and you discover what you actually built.

The mistake most founders make at this stage is one of two opposite errors:

1. They wait too long. They want everything perfect. So they polish for two more weeks, then for a third week, then a month, and the launch never quite happens.
2. They launch too loud. They blast Product Hunt, X, LinkedIn, IndieHackers, Reddit, and three Slack communities on Day 1, get a flood of low-quality signups, and learn nothing because there's no time to actually talk to anyone.

The right launch is small, fast, and intentionally ugly. You're not trying to get 10,000 users on Day 1. **You're trying to get 3 real users who will give you 30 minutes of their time.** Then 10. Then 100. The big launches come later, when you've earned the right.

---

> **Quick answer:** Launch ugly to 3 users. Then 10. Then 100. Don't blast every channel on Day 1 — you'll burn the launch and learn nothing. Start with your warmest network (people who already know you), graduate to niche communities (one at a time), and only do the big launches (Product Hunt, viral X posts) once you've earned a few testimonials and a working product. The goal of the first launch is **not traffic** — it's data, conversations, and your first paying user.

---

## The "Doing things that don't scale" mindset

YC's Stanford CS183B Lecture #8 — *"Doing Things That Don't Scale"* — is the most important launch lecture YC ever published. The framing: **at launch, your job is not to do things at scale. Your job is to do things that wouldn't work at scale, but produce extraordinary insight in small numbers.**

Justin Kan, Walker Williams, and Stanley Tang spent that lecture telling story after story of founders who hand-onboarded users one at a time. Brian Chesky personally door-knocking in NYC to get hosts on Airbnb. Stripe at hackathons literally installing their SDK on developers' laptops. DoorDash's founders manually delivering food in Palo Alto.

What looked like grunt work was actually market research at the highest possible resolution. Every conversation, every observed friction, every off-the-cuff comment from a user — all of it informed product decisions you couldn't get from analytics dashboards.

Your launch should follow the same pattern. **Hand-deliver the product to a small number of people. Watch what they do. Talk to them.** That's the launch. Anything bigger comes later.

## The launch ladder — small to large, in order

Here's the order you should launch through. Don't skip steps. Each rung earns you the next.

### Rung 1 — Your own network (Day 1–3)

**Audience size:** ~50–500 people who already know you.
**Goal:** 5–10 first signups, of which 2–3 give you real feedback.

This is the most underrated launch channel and it's free. Send a short, personal note to people you actually know — friends who fit the user profile, ex-colleagues, your LinkedIn 1st-degree connections in the right industry.

The message is something like:

> *"Hey [name], I just shipped [product]. It's a [one-line description]. I built it for [the kind of person they are or know]. If you (or someone you know) deals with [problem], would love your feedback. Live here: [link]. No pressure."*

Three rules: (1) personalize each one (no mass blasts), (2) name the problem, not the features, (3) make it easy to say no. Most won't sign up. Some will. The ones who do will give you better feedback than any large-scale launch can.

### Rung 2 — Targeted DMs to potential users (Day 3–10)

**Audience size:** 50–200 people who match your user profile but don't know you yet.
**Goal:** 10–30 conversations, ~5–15 trial users.

Same as Chapter 2's validation outreach, but now you have a real product to show. Find users in the niche communities you researched during validation. DM them individually.

> *"Hey [name], I noticed you posted about [problem] a few weeks ago. I just shipped a tool that addresses it directly. Mind if I share it with you and get your reaction? No sales pitch — I'm trying to learn what works and what doesn't."*

Founders shy away from this because it feels like sales. It isn't — at this stage you're explicitly asking for feedback, not a transaction. You'll be surprised how many people respond positively when they realize you're a real person trying to learn from them.

The output of Rung 2 is your first cohort of real, non-network users. Watch what they do. Send them follow-up emails 48 hours later.

### Rung 3 — Single niche community post (Day 7–14)

**Audience size:** 1,000–10,000 in one specific community.
**Goal:** 30–100 signups, of which 10–20 are active users.

Pick **one** community where your users live. One. Not five. Examples:

- **Indie Hackers** — for tools targeting indie founders or solo SaaS
- **A specific subreddit** — find the most-active one for your niche
- **A specific Discord/Slack** — niche professional communities (designers, developers, real estate agents, etc.)
- **A focused Facebook group** — surprisingly active for some verticals (small business owners, freelancers, parents)
- **Hacker News (Show HN)** — for technical/developer-oriented products

Write your launch post for *that specific audience.* Don't copy-paste between communities. Each post should reference the community's vocabulary, in-jokes, and known concerns.

A typical Show HN post: a one-line description of what it does, three bullets of what's interesting about it, an honest line about what's still rough, and a link. That's it. Don't oversell. The post that begs to be voted gets ignored. The post that simply describes a tool that's interesting gets traction.

### Rung 4 — Product Hunt (Week 3–4 of launch month)

**Audience size:** 50,000+ if you go to top of the page on launch day.
**Goal:** 200–1,000 signups.

Product Hunt is still alive and useful, but only if you've earned it. Going to Product Hunt with no testimonials, no early users, and no story is a wasted shot. Wait until you have:

- 5+ active users you can quote
- A short founder story (why you built this)
- A polished launch image / video
- 50+ "hunters" lined up to upvote in the first hour (your network from Rung 1)

When you're ready, the playbook is:

1. Schedule a midweek launch (Tuesday or Wednesday is best)
2. Post at 12:01 AM Pacific
3. DM your supporters in the first 30 minutes asking them to upvote and comment
4. Reply to every comment within 1 hour for the entire day
5. End the day with thank-you posts on X, LinkedIn, etc.

A typical good-but-not-amazing PH launch nets 200–500 signups, of which 5–20 will be real users. Don't expect it to make you. Expect it to widen the top of your funnel.

### Rung 5 — The "viral" post (Week 4+, optional)

**Audience size:** unpredictable.
**Goal:** unclear and unimportant.

Don't optimize for this. Viral posts on X, LinkedIn, or Reddit happen occasionally to founders who've been writing consistently. Most founders' first viral moment is unintended — a one-line tweet about something specific they noticed during the build. **You can't manufacture this.** Stop trying.

If a post does take off, ride it but don't change strategy. Conversion from viral traffic is terrible. The content of the post matters far more than the volume of views.

## What to put on your launch landing page

This is what every visitor lands on. Get it right.

```
[Your logo]
[One-line value prop — what it does, for whom]
[A 30-second loom-style demo video, OR a single screenshot]
[CTA: "Try it free" / "Book a call" — one button, primary action]

[Three benefit blocks — outcomes for the user, not features]

[A founder note — "Hi, I'm [name]. I built this because [reason]."]

[Pricing — or "Free during launch, paid plans coming soon"]

[Social proof slot — testimonials when you have them, "join the waitlist" if not]

[FAQ — 4–6 founder-anticipated questions]

[Final CTA]
```

That's the entire page. Anything more is procrastination. Anything less is unprofessional.

For copy: write it like you're talking to one specific user. Don't write "we help small businesses streamline operations." Write "if you're running a 5-person agency and you spend Sundays manually reconciling invoices, this is for you."

## Three things that increase launch conversion

Founders sweat a lot of details that don't matter. Three details that *do* matter:

### 1 — A 30-second video, not a long one

Replace screenshots with a 30-second screen-recorded demo. Show what the product does in real use. Loom or QuickTime is fine. You don't need a production crew. The video should make a stranger immediately understand what the product is and want to try it.

Long videos get watched by no one. Thirty-second videos get watched by everyone. Cut.

### 2 — Real names and faces

If you have testimonials, include the person's full name and role. "Sarah K., Marketing Manager" beats "Anonymous user." Real names trigger trust at a much higher rate than abbreviated names. Get permission, then publish.

If you don't have testimonials yet, put your own founder photo in. Putting *any* face on the page increases conversion. Faceless products feel like phishing sites.

### 3 — Reduce sign-up friction to one field

Email + password, that's it. No phone number. No "what's your role?" dropdown. No 6-step onboarding wizard. Get them inside the product, then collect the rest if you actually need it. Every additional field cuts conversion by 10–20%.

## What to expect, realistically

Founders read launch stories about products that hit 10,000 users in 24 hours and assume that's the bar. It's not. Most successful launches look like this:

- **Day 1:** 30–80 visits, 5–15 signups, 1–3 active users.
- **Week 1:** 500–2,000 visits, 50–150 signups, 10–30 active users.
- **Week 4:** 2,000–10,000 visits, 200–500 signups, 30–100 active users.
- **First paying customer:** Day 3 to Week 6, depending on how aggressively you ask.

If you're below these numbers, you have a marketing problem (Chapter 9 will help). If you're above them, congratulations — focus on retention, not more launches.

## Real example — Airbnb's New York door-knocking

In Airbnb's first year, the founders flew to New York and physically went door to door, photographing apartments for hosts and helping them write listings. They didn't have an SEO strategy. They didn't have paid ads. They had a willingness to do something that didn't scale: **personally onboard hosts, one apartment at a time.**

It worked because every host they helped became a power host. Every apartment they photographed showed better than the user-uploaded ones, which raised the bar for the entire platform. The "doesn't scale" labor created a loop: better listings → more bookings → hosts told other hosts.

You can run the same play with your product. Don't sit at home running ads. Go to where your users are — physically, virtually, however — and onboard them by hand. The first 10 users earned this way will teach you more than the next 1,000 acquired through ads.

## Your Turn

Launch week is real work. Don't skim.

1. **Make your launch list.** Three columns: people from your network (Rung 1), users from your validation interviews (Rung 2), one community you'll post to (Rung 3). Aim for 30 names in column 1, 30 in column 2, 1 in column 3.

2. **Write your three launch messages.** One for personal network, one for cold-but-warm DMs, one for the community post. Personalize the first two. Don't write the third until you've already done the first two.

3. **Build your launch landing page.** Use the template above. Don't add anything to it. Hero, demo video, three benefits, founder note, CTA. Done.

4. **Schedule your launch sequence:**
   - Day 1–3: Personal network
   - Day 3–10: Targeted DMs
   - Day 7–14: Single community post
   - Week 3–4: Product Hunt (only if you've earned it)

5. **Have a 1:1 follow-up plan.** For every signup in the first 30, send a personal note within 24 hours. *"Thanks for trying [product]. I'd love to know what you think — even if you didn't end up using it. Would you have 15 minutes this week?"* Half won't reply. The half that does will tell you exactly what to fix next.

When you have your first 5–10 active users and 2–3 conversations underway, you're ready for Chapter 8.

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>What if I have no audience and no network?</b></summary>

Then your launch is going to be slower, and that's fine. Skip Rung 1 (kind of) and lean harder on Rungs 2 and 3 — targeted DMs and a single community post. Your validation interviewees from Chapter 2 are also your launch list. That's why we tell people to keep validation interviews warm; they often become your first 5 customers.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Should I do a "soft launch" or a "hard launch"?</b></summary>

Soft launch. Always, at MVP stage. Hard launches (PH-day-one, big PR push) require a polished product and pre-existing audience. If you don't have those, a hard launch will get you brief traffic and zero retention. Soft launch ships the product, gets real users one by one, and lets you fix what's broken. Then later — once you've earned testimonials and stability — you can do a "big launch" for the same product and it'll convert.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How do I write a Product Hunt launch post that actually performs?</b></summary>

The basics: descriptive title (not clickbait), a clear "what it does" first sentence, a three-bullet "what makes this interesting" list, an authentic founder line about why you built it. Reply to every comment within an hour for the whole day. Don't beg for upvotes — most successful PH launches don't. Quality of comments matters more than quantity of upvotes for actual conversion.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What if my first launch flops?</b></summary>

Most do. The signal you should care about is not "did it go viral" — it's "did anyone use it for more than 30 seconds, and did anyone tell me what they thought?" If yes, the launch worked. If no, the problem isn't the launch — it's the product (review Chapter 5: design for clarity, and Chapter 3: scope) or the audience (review Chapter 1: are you really targeting the right user?).

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Do I need to have prices ready on Day 1?</b></summary>

Yes — even if it's a generic "Free during launch, $XX/mo coming soon." Pricing communicates that you're a real business. We dive deep on this in Chapter 8, but as a launch-prep guideline: have a number on the page. Free-forever signals "side project." A real number, even with a "free for early users" note, signals "real business."

</details>
