# Chapter 13 — Keyword Research & On-Page SEO

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/13-hero.png)

You set up your foundation in Chapter 12. Now Google can crawl you. The next question is the bigger one: *what should you write about?*

Most non-technical founders write about whatever pops into their head — "5 tips for productivity," "The future of [their industry]," random thought-leadership pieces. Six months later they have 30 blog posts and zero organic traffic. The problem isn't writing quality. The problem is they wrote about things **nobody is searching for.**

This chapter fixes that. By the end, you'll have a list of 20–50 real keywords that real humans are typing into Google **right now**, in your niche, with intent to find products like yours. You'll know which to write about first, in what order, and exactly how to structure each article so it has a shot at ranking.

---

> **Quick answer:** Keyword research is the act of finding what your customers type into Google. Use **Ahrefs Webmaster Tools (free)** + **Google's "People Also Ask"** + **AnswerThePublic** to find 20–50 long-tail keywords with low competition. Pick 3–5 to start. Write a 1,500–2,500 word article for each — with the keyword in the title, H1, first paragraph, URL, and one H2. **Match search intent**: if people are searching to *learn*, write a guide. If they're searching to *buy*, write a comparison. **Most founders fail at SEO because they write about themselves, not about what their customers Google.**

---

## The 3 keyword categories you need to understand

Not all keywords are equal. Each one tells you something about the user's stage. **Match the keyword to the right type of content** or you'll write irrelevant articles.

### Category 1 — Informational ("how to", "what is", "guide to")

Examples: *"how to build an MVP," "what is product-market fit," "best Next.js boilerplate 2026"*

**User intent:** Learning. They're not buying yet — they want education.

**What to write:** Long-form guides (1,500–3,000 words). Be the most useful answer.

**Why it matters for SaaS:** Informational traffic is the top of your funnel. People learn from your guide → become aware of your brand → return later when they're ready to buy.

### Category 2 — Commercial ("best X", "alternatives to Y", "X vs Y")

Examples: *"best landing page builders 2026," "Linear alternatives," "Webflow vs Framer"*

**User intent:** Comparing. They've decided to buy something — they're choosing between options.

**What to write:** Comparison posts, "best of" listicles, alternatives roundups.

**Why it matters for SaaS:** Commercial keywords convert dramatically higher than informational. A user who Googles *"best landing page builder for SaaS"* is 10x more likely to buy than someone Googling *"what is a landing page."*

### Category 3 — Transactional ("buy", "pricing", "[brand] login", "[your product]")

Examples: *"Pexa Studio pricing," "[your brand] login," "buy [your product]"*

**User intent:** Action. They're trying to do a thing right now.

**What to write:** Optimize your landing pages, pricing page, and brand-name pages. These are usually small, focused pages — not blog content.

**Why it matters:** This is where conversion happens. Founders sometimes overlook it because the volume is small — but every brand-name search converts at 30%+.

## The 4 free tools you need (and one paid one)

You don't need a $399/month Ahrefs subscription. Free tools cover ~80% of what an MVP-stage founder needs.

### Tool 1 — Ahrefs Webmaster Tools (free)

[https://ahrefs.com/webmaster-tools](https://ahrefs.com/webmaster-tools)

The free tier from Ahrefs (the leading paid SEO tool). Verify your domain (same DNS process as GSC) and you get:
- Keyword rankings for your site
- Backlink analysis (who's linking to you)
- "Top pages" report (which of your pages is performing best)

**Limit:** Only your own domain. Can't research competitors' keywords on free tier.

**Use it for:** Tracking your own performance and finding which keywords you're already ranking for (often a surprise — you'll have keywords you didn't know about).

### Tool 2 — Google's "People Also Ask" + Suggest

Free, built into Google. Type your seed keyword into Google. Look at:
- The "People also ask" box (always 4 questions)
- The autocomplete suggestions as you type
- The "Related searches" at the bottom of the results page

These are **literally what humans are typing.** No tool needed.

**Use it for:** Generating long-tail variations of any seed keyword in 5 minutes.

### Tool 3 — AlsoAsked (free for limited queries)

[https://alsoasked.com](https://alsoasked.com)

Aggregates Google's "People Also Ask" boxes into a tree visualization. You enter a seed keyword and see 30+ related questions, organized by topic.

**Use it for:** Finding question-based long-tail keywords — perfect for FAQ pages and blog posts.

### Tool 4 — AnswerThePublic (free with limits)

[https://answerthepublic.com](https://answerthepublic.com)

Visualizes search queries as a wheel of "who/what/where/when/why/how" + "vs/with/like" combinations.

**Use it for:** Brainstorming all the angles around a topic before you write.

### Tool 5 (paid) — Ahrefs / Semrush / Ubersuggest

If you have $50–$100/month for SEO tools, **Ahrefs ($129/month)** is the gold standard. **Ubersuggest** ($29/month) is a budget alternative. These let you research competitor keywords — which is the single biggest unlock once you have budget.

**Tip:** Don't subscribe until you've done 3–6 months on free tools. The paid tools amplify a workflow you should learn manually first.

## The keyword research workflow (90-minute exercise)

This is the workbook. Do it right now.

### Step 1 — Brainstorm 5 seed keywords (10 min)

A "seed keyword" is the broadest term someone searching for your product might use. Write 5.

For Pexa Studio, examples:
- "MVP development"
- "non-technical founder"
- "SaaS launch"
- "Next.js development agency"
- "21 day MVP"

For your product, what 5 broad terms describe what you do?

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"My product is [one-line description]. My target customer is [persona]. Generate 10 broad seed keywords they might type into Google when looking for a product like mine. Mix product-category terms, problem terms, and outcome terms."*

### Step 2 — Expand each seed into long-tail keywords (30 min)

For each seed, find 10 long-tail variations.

**Method A — Google directly.** Type your seed. Read autocomplete suggestions. Hit search. Read "People Also Ask." Read "Related searches" at the bottom. Write down everything that's relevant.

**Method B — AlsoAsked.** Paste your seed keyword. Get a tree of question-based keywords.

**Method C — AnswerThePublic.** Same idea, different visualization.

After 30 minutes, you should have a list of 50+ specific phrases.

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Here's my seed keyword: '[keyword]'. Generate 30 long-tail variations a real user might type into Google. Include question forms (how/what/why/when), comparison forms (X vs Y), and modifier forms (best/cheap/free)."*

### Step 3 — Filter for intent and competition (20 min)

For each keyword, ask:

1. **What's the intent?** Informational, commercial, or transactional?
2. **What kind of content ranks for this?** Google it. Are the top 10 results blog posts, comparison articles, or product pages?
3. **Can I realistically rank?** If the top 10 results are all from sites with 100,000+ monthly visitors, skip it. Find a more specific variation.

**The sweet spot for MVP-stage founders: long-tail informational keywords with 100–1,000 monthly searches and low competition.** Your traffic per article will be modest, but you can win them.

### Step 4 — Pick your first 5 articles (15 min)

From your filtered list, pick 5 keywords:

- Mix categories: 3 informational + 2 commercial
- Pick keywords where you have a unique angle or experience
- Pick keywords your customer is realistically Googling

Write down: *"Article 1: [title] / target keyword: [keyword] / intent: [info|commercial]"*

### Step 5 — Set a publishing cadence (15 min)

Realistic cadence options:
- **Aggressive:** 2 articles/week (8/month). For founders who can write fast.
- **Standard:** 1 article/week (4/month). For most.
- **Minimum:** 1 article every 2 weeks (2/month). The floor below which SEO isn't a real channel for you.

Pick one. Block it on your calendar. **The founders who win at SEO are the ones who keep posting after the first 6 months produced almost no results.**

## On-page SEO — how to actually write the article

Once you have your keyword and your topic, here's how to structure the page so it has a chance at ranking.

### The 8 on-page elements that matter most

#### 1. URL slug

Use the keyword. Short, lowercase, hyphenated.

Good: `yourcompany.com/blog/how-to-build-mvp-21-days`
Bad: `yourcompany.com/blog/post?id=4839`

#### 2. Page title (the `<title>` tag)

Include your keyword near the start. Keep under 60 characters (Google truncates longer).

Good: *"How to Build an MVP in 21 Days — Pexa Studio"*
Bad: *"Some Thoughts on Modern Product Development"*

#### 3. Meta description

The 1–2 sentence preview Google shows in search results. Include the keyword. Keep under 160 characters.

#### 4. H1 (the page heading)

Contains the keyword. Should match the user's search intent.

#### 5. First 100 words

Mention the keyword in the first paragraph. Don't stuff it — just include it naturally.

#### 6. H2 subheadings

At least one H2 should include the keyword or a close variant.

#### 7. Internal links

Link to 3–5 other pages on your own site within the article. This is the most underrated SEO move. It signals topical authority and keeps readers on your site.

#### 8. Outbound links

Link to 2–3 external high-authority sources (Wikipedia, official docs, established publications). This builds your credibility and helps with "E-E-A-T" (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust — Google's quality signals).

> **AI shortcut:** Once you have your draft, run it through [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Here's a blog post draft on [topic] targeting the keyword '[keyword]'. Audit it for on-page SEO: is the keyword in the title, H1, URL, first 100 words, and at least one H2? Any awkward keyword stuffing? Suggest improvements without rewriting the voice."*

## Article length — how long is long enough?

Modern Google rewards comprehensive content but punishes filler. The shape:

- **Minimum:** 1,000 words. Below this, you'll lose to longer articles by default.
- **Sweet spot:** 1,500–2,500 words. Long enough to be authoritative, short enough to read.
- **Maximum useful:** 4,000 words for cornerstone "ultimate guide" pieces. Beyond that, attention drops.

The Pexa Studio blog post on [MVP development cost](https://pexastudio.com/blog/mvp-development-cost) is ~3,400 words and ranks for several long-tail keywords. That's a good template length.

## Match the format to the keyword type

This is where most founders fail. They write the wrong format for the keyword.

| If keyword type is... | Write format... |
|----------------------|-----------------|
| "how to X" | Step-by-step tutorial |
| "what is X" | Definitional + use cases |
| "X vs Y" | Comparison table + opinion |
| "best X for [use case]" | Listicle, 7–10 options, with pros/cons |
| "X alternatives" | List of alternatives with detailed descriptions |
| "X tutorial" | Tutorial with code/screenshots |
| "X cost / pricing" | Cost breakdown with real numbers |
| "X guide" | Comprehensive long-form |

If you Google your target keyword and the top 10 results are listicles, **write a listicle.** Don't write an essay. Match the format Google has already decided is correct.

## Internal linking strategy

After your 5th article, internal linking becomes a superpower.

Rule: **every new article should link to 3–5 older articles on related topics.** Conversely, go back to older articles and add links to your newer ones.

This forms a "topic cluster" — Google sees that you've written deeply on a subject and treats you as authoritative. The result: your articles rank higher than they "should" based on backlinks alone.

A simple internal-linking structure for an MVP-focused blog:

```
Pillar page: "How to build an MVP" (3000 words)
  ├── Sub-article: "How long does it take to build an MVP?"
  ├── Sub-article: "How much does an MVP cost?"
  ├── Sub-article: "Best stack for building an MVP in 2026"
  ├── Sub-article: "MVP vs prototype — what's the difference?"
  └── Sub-article: "How to validate an MVP idea"
```

Each sub-article links back to the pillar. The pillar links forward to each sub-article. **All 6 pages strengthen each other.**

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Here's my list of 10 published blog posts: [list with URLs]. Suggest a topic cluster structure — which is the pillar, which are sub-articles, and which internal links should I add to each."*

## Real example — Pexa Studio's blog architecture

Pexa Studio currently has one main article ([MVP development cost](https://pexastudio.com/blog/mvp-development-cost)) and is building out a topic cluster around it. The plan:

- **Pillar:** "How much does it cost to build an MVP?" (live)
- **Sub-articles being written:**
  - "How long does an MVP actually take?"
  - "MVP vs prototype vs full product — what's the difference?"
  - "Best tech stack for MVP in 2026"
  - "How to validate an MVP before you build"
- **Internal linking:** Every sub-article will link to the pillar; the pillar will be updated to link to each sub-article as they publish

This is the structure that wins on a 6–12 month horizon.

## Your Turn — the keyword + on-page workbook

Don't read on. Open these tabs and do the work.

1. **Open** [Google Sheets](https://sheets.new) — create a "Keyword Research" sheet with columns: *Keyword, Intent (info/commercial/transactional), Format, Estimated Volume, Competition, Priority*

2. **Run Step 1** above. Brainstorm 5 seed keywords. Type them into row 1.

3. **Run Step 2.** For each seed, generate 10 long-tail variations using AlsoAsked + Google + Prompt It. Add them to your sheet.

4. **Run Step 3.** Tag each with intent and competition. Cross out the impossibly competitive ones.

5. **Run Step 4.** Pick 5 articles to write first. Highlight them.

6. **Set your publishing cadence.** 1 article/week. Block it.

7. **Write your first article** following the on-page checklist above.

8. **Publish it.** Submit the new URL to Google Search Console (Sitemaps → Inspect URL → Request Indexing).

9. **Wait 1–2 weeks.** Check GSC for impressions on your target keyword. If ranked: celebrate. If not: write another article. Repeat.

When you've published 5 articles and one of them is showing impressions in GSC, you're ready for Chapter 14 — programmatic SEO at scale.

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>How long does an article take to rank?</b></summary>

For long-tail, low-competition keywords on a new site: 2–8 weeks to first appearance, 3–6 months to settle into a stable position. For higher-competition keywords: 6–12 months minimum, often longer. SEO is the slowest channel. Don't measure success at week 4. Measure at month 6.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Should I update old articles?</b></summary>

Yes. Updating > publishing new most of the time. Once an article has been live for 12+ months, refreshing it (new examples, updated stats, expanded sections) often doubles its traffic. Google rewards "freshness signals." Update one old article every 2 weeks.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Do I need backlinks?</b></summary>

For low-competition long-tail keywords: no, you can rank with just on-page SEO. For competitive head terms: yes, eventually. But backlink-building is its own discipline that's mostly post-MVP. Focus on content + on-page first. Backlinks come naturally when content is good and you do enough distribution (Chapters 16–20).

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What about voice search / AI search?</b></summary>

Voice search is mostly the same as text search at this point — same keywords, same intent. AI search (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude) is genuinely different and we cover it deeply in Chapter 15. The short version: optimize for being *cited* by AI, not just for ranking in Google.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Should I use AI to write the articles?</b></summary>

You can use AI to *draft* articles, but published as-is, AI content underperforms. The pattern that works: AI generates the structure + first draft, you rewrite the most important sections in your voice with real examples and opinions. Hybrid AI-human content outranks both pure-AI and pure-human content as of 2026.

</details>
