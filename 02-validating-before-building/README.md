# Chapter 2 — Validating Before You Build

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/02-hero.png)

Here's the embarrassing math: most non-technical founders spend three to nine months building something that takes about **two days of conversation** to determine they shouldn't have built.

Two days. That's the cost of validation. The cost of skipping it is your savings, your weekends, your relationship with whoever you talked into helping you, and, occasionally, your career.

This chapter is about the cheapest, fastest, most uncomfortable thing you'll do as a founder: talk to ten real human beings about your idea before you write a single line of code.

---

> **Quick answer:** Validation is not "asking your friends if they like the idea." It's structured conversations with 10 real users in your target market, designed to discover **how they currently solve the problem**, not whether they like your solution. Done right, it takes 2–3 days and saves you 3–9 months. The only correct outcome is one of two: (a) clear evidence to build, or (b) clear evidence to pivot. If you finish validation feeling "kind of optimistic," you didn't validate.

---

## Why founders skip this (and why it kills them)

The reason validation gets skipped isn't laziness. It's three uncomfortable truths:

1. **It feels slow.** You want to be building. Talking to people feels like procrastination.
2. **It risks the dream.** What if they say it's a bad idea? Now you've killed your own project before it lived.
3. **It's awkward.** Cold-DMing strangers to ask about their problems is not how most people spend their Tuesday.

These are real. They're also the exact reasons why **the founders who do this win, and the ones who don't lose**. The discomfort is the price of admission. You're not going to feel cool doing it. You're going to feel like a slightly weird person sending awkward messages to strangers. Do it anyway.

YC's Emmett Shear (CEO of Twitch, formerly), in Stanford CS183B Lecture #16, taught an entire class on user interviews. The core point: *"The goal is not to pitch. The goal is to learn."* Founders who pitch during validation get back nothing useful. Founders who listen get a complete map of what to build.

## The two kinds of validation, and why one is bullshit

There's a fake version and a real version of validation. Most founders do the fake version, then are shocked when their launch flops.

**Fake validation:** asking people *"would you use this?"* They say yes. You build it. Nobody uses it.

**Real validation:** asking people *"how do you currently deal with [problem]? Walk me through the last time it happened. What did you spend, in time or money?"* They tell you. You either find an opportunity or you don't.

The reason the fake version doesn't work is that humans are wired to be polite. When you describe your idea, they pattern-match it onto their own life and politely say *"sure, that sounds useful."* They are not lying — they are just answering a hypothetical, which has zero predictive power.

The real version doesn't ask hypotheticals. It asks about **past behavior** in concrete situations. Past behavior is the only reliable predictor of future behavior. Period.

## The "Mom Test" filter

Rob Fitzpatrick wrote a tiny book called *The Mom Test* whose central insight is this: **a good user-research question is one your mom can't lie to you about.**

Bad question: *"Would you pay $30/month for an app that organized your grocery list across stores?"*
- Your mom says: "Of course, honey, that sounds wonderful." (Useless.)

Good question: *"Walk me through how you bought groceries this week. Where did you start? What did you use? What was annoying?"*
- Your mom says: "Well, I started on Tuesday and went to two stores because Trader Joe's didn't have… [continues for 8 minutes]." (Gold.)

The first question asks about a hypothetical future. The second asks about real past behavior. Always go for the second.

## The 10-interview script

Here's the exact script for one validation interview. Each one takes 20–30 minutes. You'll do ten of these. If you space them, you can finish in 2–3 days.

### Step 1 — Get them on the call (30 sec)

Open with a frame that lowers their expectations. *"Thanks for hopping on. I'm researching how people deal with [problem]. I'm not selling anything — I just want to learn what's working and what's broken for you. I'll ask 5–6 questions and you can stop any time."*

This frame is critical. The moment they think you're selling, they shift into polite mode. Once they're in polite mode, you get useless answers.

### Step 2 — Anchor in the past (2 min)

*"Walk me through the last time you ran into [problem]. When was it? What were you trying to do?"*

Their job here is to remember a specific event. Yours is to shut up and let them remember.

### Step 3 — Trace what they did (5–10 min)

This is where the gold lives. Once they're describing a real event, ask:

- *"What did you try first?"*
- *"Why didn't that work?"*
- *"What did you do next?"*
- *"How long did the whole thing take?"*
- *"Did you spend any money trying to fix it?"*

You're building a map of their **current workflow** for this problem. Every step in that workflow is a chance for your product to slot in.

### Step 4 — Find the breaking point (3 min)

*"What was the most frustrating moment in that whole experience?"*

This question is a gift. It tells you where the highest emotional pain is — which is exactly where your product should focus.

### Step 5 — Test their willingness to pay (3 min)

*"You mentioned you spent [time/money] trying to deal with this. If something fixed it for you, what would that be worth to you per month?"*

You're not pitching here. You're letting them tell you their own price. People are remarkably accurate when you ask them what they'd pay for a *specific* solution to a *specific* pain they just described.

If they hesitate or say "I don't know, $5?" — that's a real signal. Most people don't pay for "$5/month nice-to-haves." Look for $30+/month answers, or one-time payments of $200+, before you get excited.

### Step 6 — Get the next interview (1 min)

*"This was super helpful. Do you know two other people who run into this problem? I'd love to talk to them too."*

Founders forget to ask this. It's the single most efficient way to fill your interview pipeline. Three out of five people will give you names.

### Step 7 — Take notes immediately (5 min after)

The moment the call ends, before you do anything else, write down:
- What they said in their own words (verbatim if possible)
- The most painful moment they described
- What they currently spend (time + money)
- Whether they sounded genuinely frustrated or just mildly annoyed
- Whether their language matched what you assumed about your customer

These notes are your dataset. Don't skip this step.

## Where to find your 10 interviewees

A common founder block: *"Where do I even find these people?"* Here are the four channels that work, in order of speed:

### Channel 1 — Your own network (24–48 hours)

Send a message to 30 people in your contacts: *"Hey — I'm researching [problem] and looking for 5–10 people to chat with for 20 minutes. Do you know anyone who deals with this regularly? Would love an intro."*

Three out of every ten people will respond with at least one name. That's nine intros from one batch.

### Channel 2 — Reddit / niche communities (3–5 days)

Find the subreddit, Discord, or Slack where your target users hang out. Don't post a "I'm doing market research" thread (those get downvoted). DM individual users who've posted about the problem in the last 30 days. *"Hey, saw your post about X. Mind if I ask you about it for 15 minutes?"* Expect a 10–20% response rate.

### Channel 3 — LinkedIn cold outreach (3–7 days)

Search by job title or industry. Send a personalized 2-line message — not a sales pitch, a research request. *"Hi [name], I'm doing research on how [their role] deal with [problem]. Would you have 15 minutes to share your experience? Happy to send you a $20 Amazon gift card for your time."* The gift card raises response rates from 5% to ~25%.

### Channel 4 — Indie Hackers / X / Twitter (7+ days)

Slowest but highest quality if you have any audience. Post asking for interviewees. The people who volunteer are pre-filtered — they care about the problem enough to respond.

## What "validated" actually looks like

You've done the 10 interviews. How do you know if you've validated?

**Strong validation (build it):**
- 7+ out of 10 described the same painful moment
- 5+ already pay money or invest significant time trying to solve it
- 3+ asked you, unprompted, *"when can I try it?"* during the interview
- The price points they mentioned cluster in the same range

**Weak validation (rework or pivot):**
- People understand the problem but don't sound emotionally activated by it
- Most have "kind of solved it" with workarounds they don't mind
- Price answers are all over the place (or all under $10/month)
- Nobody asks you when they can try it

**No validation (kill the idea):**
- People don't recognize the problem as a problem
- They've never tried to solve it
- They wouldn't pay for a fix
- You're now arguing with their answers in your head

The third outcome is brutal. It's also the most valuable result you can possibly get. Killing a bad idea now saves you 3–9 months. **A killed idea is a saved year.**

## Real example — Stripe's "Collison Installation"

Before Stripe was a company, the Collison brothers were just two devs who hated how hard it was to accept payments online. They didn't write a business plan. They went and talked to other developers — friends, friends of friends, anyone they could corner.

What they discovered wasn't surprising in retrospect: developers all had the same complaint. Existing payment processors required pages of documentation, lengthy approval processes, and integrations that took weeks. Every developer they spoke to had a story about losing days to it.

So Stripe built the smallest version of a fix: seven lines of code to accept payments. They didn't launch with a brand. They didn't launch with marketing. They walked up to other devs at meetups and *physically installed Stripe on their laptops*. This is what the YC universe calls the "Collison Installation" — and it's a textbook example of validation working backward into product.

The lesson: they weren't selling. They were watching what developers actually did when handed a tool, and listening to what tripped them up. The product itself was the validation.

## Your Turn

This week. Not next week. This week.

1. **Identify your target user in one sentence.** Not "small business owners." Try "marketing managers at e-commerce companies with $1M–$10M in annual revenue." If you can't be that specific, your idea is still too vague.

2. **Make a list of 30 people you could potentially talk to.** Network, LinkedIn, Reddit, Discord. Don't filter by who'll say yes. List everyone.

3. **Send 30 outreach messages this week.** Use the 2-line script above. Aim for 10 confirmed interviews.

4. **Write your interview script.** Use the 7 steps above as your skeleton. Customize the questions to your specific problem domain.

5. **Do all 10 interviews in one week if possible.** Compression matters — patterns become obvious when interviews are close together. If they're spread over two months, you'll forget what you heard.

6. **At the end of week 1, write a one-page summary:**
   - What problem do they actually have? (in their words)
   - What are they currently doing about it?
   - What would they pay?
   - Is there a real opportunity here, or not?

If the answer is *yes*, move to Chapter 3.
If the answer is *no*, go back to Chapter 1 with the lesson you just learned. Pick a different idea. Repeat.

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>What if I'm too shy / introverted to do user interviews?</b></summary>

You're not. You're just untrained. The first interview is uncomfortable. The third is fine. The seventh is interesting. By number ten, you'll wish you'd started doing this years ago. Discomfort fades faster than you think — but only if you actually do them.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Can I use surveys instead of interviews?</b></summary>

No. Surveys produce structured data from people answering hypotheticals — exactly what you don't want at this stage. The point of interviews is to hear stories, follow tangents, and discover things you didn't know to ask. Surveys can't do that. After you have a working product, surveys are useful for measurement. Not now.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How do I find interviewees in a market I'm not part of?</b></summary>

Slowly. This is exactly why founders who pick problems they've personally felt have an enormous advantage. If you must enter a market you don't know, expect validation to take 4–6 weeks instead of 2–3 days, and budget for the gift cards or interview fees you'll need to compensate strangers.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What if interviewees love the idea but won't commit to paying?</b></summary>

Then they don't really love it. "Love but won't pay" is a polite version of "doesn't actually want it." Don't romanticize verbal enthusiasm. Watch what they're already paying money for or spending time on. That's where the real demand is.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How many interviews is "enough"?</b></summary>

Ten is the bare minimum to spot patterns. Twenty is when you have real confidence. If after 10 interviews the answers are all over the place — different problems, different price points, different workflows — keep going until the patterns clarify or you decide the market is too fragmented to attack as one product.

</details>
