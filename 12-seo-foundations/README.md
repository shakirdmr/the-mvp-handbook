# Chapter 12 — SEO Foundations & Google Search Console

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/12-hero.png)

If your product is on the internet and you don't have Google Search Console set up, you are flying blind. You are also missing the single highest-ROI distribution channel that exists for SaaS — the one that compounds for years and brings users to you while you sleep.

Most non-technical founders look at "SEO" as a vague black box maintained by a $500/hour consultant. **It's not.** SEO is a stack of tools and habits. Once you set up the stack and learn the habits, you can run it yourself. This chapter does that setup, end to end, in roughly 90 minutes of your time.

This is **the most workbook-style chapter in the entire library.** Every section ends with a concrete task. Don't skip them. By the end, you'll have your domain submitted to Google, your sitemap live, your analytics tracking, and your first three keywords identified. Then Chapter 13 takes you from setup → strategy.

---

> **Quick answer:** SEO foundations are 5 tools and 4 hours of one-time setup: **Google Search Console** (track how Google sees you), **Bing Webmaster Tools** (yes, Bing matters in 2026 because of AI search), **a sitemap.xml** (auto-generated by Next.js or your CMS), **a robots.txt + llms.txt** (tell crawlers what to index), and **Plausible or PostHog** for analytics. Submit your sitemap to Google, verify your domain, and you're indexed within days. Skip this and your blog posts won't show up for months — even if they're brilliant.

## Why this chapter is the boring one (and why that's good)

Most founders skip SEO foundations because they look "technical" and unsexy. You'll see SEO advice that says "create great content!" and skips straight to the writing part. That advice is incomplete. Great content that isn't crawlable, indexable, and submitted **doesn't rank.** I've watched founders publish 30 brilliant blog posts that earned them zero organic traffic because their `robots.txt` was misconfigured.

The setup work in this chapter is unglamorous, but it's also one-and-done. You spend 90 minutes once. After that, every blog post and every PSEO page (Chapter 14) gets indexed automatically and starts competing for traffic.

If you want the boring chapter to be over fast: **don't skim. Do each task as you read it.** Open new tabs. Type the URLs. By the end of the chapter your foundation is set forever.

## What "indexed" means and why it's everything

"Indexed" means Google has crawled your page, read it, and added it to its index — the giant database it searches when someone types a query. **Pages that aren't indexed cannot rank.** Pages that aren't ranked don't bring traffic.

The default state of a new website is: not indexed. Google doesn't know you exist. You have to tell it. The mechanism for telling it is **Google Search Console**, and the way to make it find your pages is a **sitemap.xml**.

That's the whole foundational loop:
1. You publish a page
2. Your sitemap auto-updates
3. Google reads the sitemap (because GSC is connected)
4. Google indexes the new page
5. Page becomes available to rank

When this loop is broken (missing sitemap, blocked by robots.txt, or just no GSC), every page you publish is invisible. Founders publish their hearts out and wonder why no traffic shows up. Almost always it's the loop.

## Workbook task 1 — Google Search Console (15 min)

This is non-negotiable. Do it now.

**Step 1.** Go to [https://search.google.com/search-console](https://search.google.com/search-console)

**Step 2.** Click **Start now** → sign in with the Google account you want to associate with this property.

**Step 3.** When asked for property type, choose **Domain** (not URL prefix). Domain covers all subdomains and protocols at once.

**Step 4.** Enter your domain (e.g. `yourcompany.com`). Click **Continue**.

**Step 5.** Google will ask you to verify ownership via DNS. Copy the TXT record they show you.

**Step 6.** Go to your DNS provider (Cloudflare, Vercel, Namecheap, GoDaddy, wherever you bought the domain). Add a new **TXT record** with the value Google gave you.

**Step 7.** Wait 1–10 minutes. Click **Verify** in Search Console.

**Done.** You now have Search Console connected to your domain. Bookmark it. Check it weekly.

Walk me through adding a TXT record for Google Search Console verification, step by step."* Run it. Use the cleaned prompt in ChatGPT or Claude.

## Workbook task 2 — Bing Webmaster Tools (5 min)

Most founders skip Bing. Don't. Two reasons:

1. Bing powers ~10% of US desktop search and is growing.
2. **More importantly: ChatGPT search uses Bing's index.** If you want ChatGPT to cite your site (Chapter 15 covers this in depth), you need to be in Bing's index.

**Step 1.** Go to [https://www.bing.com/webmasters](https://www.bing.com/webmasters)

**Step 2.** Sign in. Click **Add a site**.

**Step 3.** **Shortcut:** Bing offers an "Import from Google Search Console" option. Click it. Authorize. Bing imports everything in 30 seconds.

If GSC import doesn't work, use the same DNS verification process as Step 6 above with the value Bing provides.

## Workbook task 3 — Submit your sitemap (10 min)

A **sitemap.xml** is a file at `yourcompany.com/sitemap.xml` that lists every page on your site. It tells crawlers what exists and how often it updates.

If you used the modern stack from Chapter 4 (Next.js + Vercel), **your sitemap is already there.** Next.js auto-generates one if you have a `sitemap.ts` file in your `app/` directory. Pexa Studio's site has one — see [`pexastudio.com/sitemap.xml`](https://pexastudio.com/sitemap.xml).

**Step 1.** Open your browser and visit `https://yourcompany.com/sitemap.xml`. You should see XML listing your pages. If you don't:
- **Next.js:** Create `src/app/sitemap.ts` with a default-export function that returns an array of `{ url, lastModified }` entries. Next.js does the rest. (See [Next.js docs](https://nextjs.org/docs/app/api-reference/file-conventions/metadata/sitemap).)
- **Webflow / Framer:** Both auto-generate. Find the URL in their settings.
- **WordPress:** Install the **Yoast SEO** plugin. It generates `sitemap_index.xml`.

**Step 2.** Once your sitemap is live, in Google Search Console click **Sitemaps** in the left nav.

**Step 3.** Enter `sitemap.xml` and click **Submit**.

**Step 4.** In Bing Webmaster Tools, do the same: **Sitemaps → Submit sitemap**.

**Step 5.** Within 24 hours, you'll see Google start indexing your pages. Most pages are indexed within 1–7 days.

> **AI shortcut:** If you're on a custom stack and not sure how to generate a sitemap, paste your tech stack into [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) with: *"How do I auto-generate a sitemap.xml for a [your stack] site? I want it to update whenever I publish a new page."* The cleaned prompt → ChatGPT → working code in 60 seconds.

## Workbook task 4 — robots.txt + llms.txt (10 min)

These are two tiny text files that live at the root of your domain. They control what crawlers can and cannot read.

### robots.txt

Tells search engine crawlers (Google, Bing) what's open and what's closed.

A reasonable default:
```
User-agent: *
Allow: /

Sitemap: https://yourcompany.com/sitemap.xml
```

**Step 1.** Visit `https://yourcompany.com/robots.txt`. If you see this kind of content, you're set.

**Step 2.** If it's missing or blocking everything (`Disallow: /`), fix it. Next.js: add `src/app/robots.ts`. WordPress: edit via Yoast. Webflow: Project Settings → SEO. Framer: Project Settings → robots.txt.

### llms.txt

A newer file (~2024) that's the AI-search equivalent of robots.txt. It tells LLMs and AI search engines what content is canonical and worth citing.

A simple template (place at `yourcompany.com/llms.txt`):
```
# Pexa Studio

> SaaS launch partner that takes non-technical founders from idea to live product in 21 days.

## Core Pages

- [About](https://pexastudio.com/about)
- [Services](https://pexastudio.com/#services)
- [Pricing](https://pexastudio.com/#pricing)
- [Blog](https://pexastudio.com/blog)
- [Book a call](https://cal.com/shakirdmr/free-intro-call)

## What we do

We build SaaS MVPs in 21 days for fixed prices ($3k–$6k). Stack: Next.js, Supabase, Stripe, Vercel.
```

This file is becoming the standard for AI crawlers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Perplexity all read it). Pexa Studio's site already has one — check `pexastudio.com/llms.txt` for the live example.

**Step 3.** Create or update `llms.txt` for your site. Put it at the root.

We'll come back to this in Chapter 15 (GEO/AEO) with deeper structure.

## Workbook task 5 — Analytics (15 min)

You can't improve what you don't measure. Pick **one** analytics tool and install it.

The two best options for non-technical founders:

### Plausible Analytics — recommended for most

[https://plausible.io](https://plausible.io)

- **Cost:** $9/month for up to 10,000 monthly visitors
- **Pros:** Cookie-free (no GDPR banner). One-line install. Beautiful dashboard. Privacy-friendly.
- **Cons:** Less granular than PostHog
- **Best for:** Marketing sites, blogs, content-driven SaaS

**Step 1.** Sign up at plausible.io. Add your domain. Copy the script tag.
**Step 2.** Paste it in your `<head>` (Next.js: in `src/app/layout.tsx`).
**Step 3.** Deploy. Visit your site once. Plausible should show 1 visitor.

### PostHog — recommended for product-led founders

[https://posthog.com](https://posthog.com)

- **Cost:** Free tier covers up to 1M events/month
- **Pros:** Tracks marketing AND product analytics in one tool. Heatmaps, session replays, funnels, A/B testing.
- **Cons:** More features = more setup
- **Best for:** SaaS where you want to track marketing conversion AND in-app behavior

**Step 1.** Sign up at posthog.com. Choose region (EU or US).
**Step 2.** Install via npm (`npm install posthog-js`) or copy their snippet into `<head>`.
**Step 3.** Deploy. Verify events fire in their dashboard.

> **AI shortcut:** Need help installing on your specific stack? [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"I'm using [Next.js / WordPress / Webflow / etc.]. Walk me through installing [Plausible / PostHog] step by step, with the exact code I need to paste."* Saves 30 minutes of doc-hunting.

## Workbook task 6 — Schema markup (15 min)

**Schema** is structured data you embed in your HTML that tells Google (and AI search) exactly what your content is. Pages with schema get richer results — star ratings, FAQ accordions, breadcrumbs in search results.

Most non-technical founders skip this. **Don't.** It takes 15 minutes and meaningfully boosts click-through rates.

The 4 schema types every SaaS site should have:

### 1. Organization schema (your homepage)

Identifies you as a business. Goes on the homepage. Pexa Studio uses this.

### 2. WebPage schema (every page)

Wraps each page with metadata.

### 3. FAQPage schema (your FAQ + chapters with FAQs)

Turns FAQs into rich results in Google. Massive CTR boost. Both `mvp-development-cost.tsx` and the public Pexa Studio site use this.

### 4. Article schema (every blog post)

Marks blog content as articles, eligible for Google News Discover and "Top stories" carousels.

**Step 1.** Use **Schema.org's** generator at [https://schema.org](https://schema.org) or **Google's Rich Results Test** at [https://search.google.com/test/rich-results](https://search.google.com/test/rich-results) to validate your schema.

**Step 2.** In Next.js, use the existing pattern Pexa Studio uses — see [`src/app/page.tsx`](https://github.com/shakirdmr/pexastudio/blob/main/src/app/page.tsx) for the `organizationSchema` constant injected via JSON-LD `<script>` tag.

**Step 3.** Add Organization schema to your homepage. FAQ schema to your FAQ page. Article schema to each blog post.

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Generate JSON-LD schema markup for [Organization / FAQPage / Article] for a SaaS site at [your domain]. Include the recommended fields per Google's documentation. Format as a script tag I can paste into my Next.js app/layout.tsx."* Saves 90% of the schema-writing time.

## How long until you see traffic?

The honest timeline:

- **Week 1:** Pages start appearing in GSC's "Coverage" report. Most show "Indexed."
- **Week 2–4:** First impressions appear in Performance report. Maybe 50–500 impressions/week, mostly your brand name.
- **Month 2–3:** First non-brand impressions if you've published a few articles. Click-through rate is usually low (1–3%) at first.
- **Month 3–6:** First real organic traffic. ~50–500 clicks/month. Some long-tail keywords starting to rank.
- **Month 6–12:** If you've kept publishing, you should be at 1,000–10,000 clicks/month. Real authority building.
- **Year 2:** Compounds. The articles from month 1 are now ranking on page 1. New articles rank in weeks instead of months.

**SEO is the slowest distribution channel and the most durable.** A blog post you wrote in January 2026 can be sending you traffic in 2030. Worth the wait.

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>Do I need to do this if I'm using Webflow / Framer / Wordpress?</b></summary>

Yes. The platforms make some of this automatic (sitemap generation, basic schema), but you still need to verify Google Search Console, submit your sitemap manually, and add llms.txt. Don't assume the platform did it for you. Visit `yourcompany.com/sitemap.xml` and `yourcompany.com/robots.txt` in your browser. If they 404, the platform didn't generate them.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What's the difference between schema, structured data, and JSON-LD?</b></summary>

Schema is the *vocabulary* (the data types defined at schema.org). Structured data is the *concept* of marking up content. JSON-LD is the *format* most often used to write structured data using schema vocabulary. Practically: you'll write JSON-LD blocks in `<script>` tags that follow schema.org's data types. They're three names for the same thing.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Should I worry about page speed / Core Web Vitals?</b></summary>

Yes, but it's mostly free if you're on Vercel + Next.js. The defaults are fast. Run [PageSpeed Insights](https://pagespeed.web.dev) on your homepage. If it's >85 on mobile, you're fine. If it's <70, fix it before publishing more content.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How often should I check Google Search Console?</b></summary>

Weekly, for 5 minutes. Look at: (1) Performance — what queries are bringing impressions, (2) Coverage — any pages with errors, (3) Core Web Vitals — any URLs flagged as "needs improvement." Fix what's broken, ignore the rest. Don't obsess over daily traffic numbers in the first 6 months.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What if my site is deindexed?</b></summary>

Most "deindexed" cases are actually "not yet indexed." Use GSC's URL Inspection tool — paste a URL, it'll tell you if Google has crawled and indexed it. If status is "URL is not on Google," click **Request Indexing** to manually trigger a crawl. If you've been blocking Google in robots.txt by accident, fix that first.

</details>
