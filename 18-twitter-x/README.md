# Chapter 18 — Twitter / X: Build in Public

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/18-hero.png)

There's a specific kind of founder for whom Twitter (now X) is the single most valuable distribution channel that exists: **the technical founder, the indie hacker, the dev tool, the AI product, the developer-facing SaaS.** If your customer is anyone who reads developer Twitter, *every other channel is wrong* before this one.

For everyone else — non-technical founders building B2B SaaS — Twitter is a secondary channel, useful but not primary. LinkedIn (Chapter 17) will outperform.

This chapter is for the founder who fits Twitter's audience. **The "build in public" playbook turned dozens of indie founders into seven-figure businesses since 2020**, and the platform is still wide open in 2026. We'll cover what that playbook actually looks like, day by day.

---

> **Quick answer:** Twitter / X works for founders building dev tools, AI products, indie SaaS, and anything technical. The "build in public" playbook: post your daily progress, share specific numbers (revenue, signups, code commits), engage with bigger accounts in your niche, and use threads to dive deeper on what's working. **3 months of daily posting reliably gets you 1,000 followers and your first inbound users.** Reply > tweet > thread, in that order of frequency. Founders who win on Twitter are the ones who spend 80% of their time engaging with others' content and 20% creating their own.

---

## Who Twitter is right for (and who should skip it)

Be honest about whether Twitter is worth your time.

### Twitter is RIGHT for you if:

- Your customer is a developer, product manager, designer, AI researcher, or technical founder
- Your product is a dev tool, AI tool, indie SaaS, design tool, productivity tool
- You enjoy short-form, opinionated writing
- Your TAM is global / English-speaking / online
- Your audience reads tech news on Twitter

### Twitter is WRONG for you if:

- Your customer is a non-technical small-business owner (LinkedIn or Facebook is better)
- Your customer is a teenager or Gen-Z consumer (TikTok / YouTube Shorts is better)
- You're targeting a specific industry that's not tech (LinkedIn industry-specific or relevant Reddit)
- You hate writing
- Your local market is the priority (regional channels are better)

If Twitter is wrong for you, skip this chapter and double down on LinkedIn (Ch 17), Reddit (Ch 16), or short-form video (Ch 19).

If Twitter is right for you, what follows is the only chapter you need on it.

## The "build in public" thesis

"Build in public" = sharing your founder journey in real-time. The metrics, the wins, the failures, the products you ship, the bugs you find.

It works because:

1. **Specificity earns trust.** *"I made $1,847 MRR last month"* beats any cold marketing claim.
2. **Process is more valuable than outcome.** People follow founders for the *journey*, not just the destination — meaning you don't need to be successful yet to attract followers.
3. **Compounding network.** Twitter's reply-and-retweet structure means every conversation adds new viewers. After 6 months of consistent posting, your reach grows without you doing more work.
4. **Higher trust = higher conversion.** Inbound users from Twitter convert at 3–5x the rate of paid traffic, because they've been "watching" you for weeks before they sign up.

Founders like Daniel Vassallo (~$1M+ from Gumroad), Pieter Levels (~$1M+ from indie projects), Justin Welsh (~$5M+ from solopreneur content), and the Cursor team built their entire businesses on this loop.

You can do a smaller version. Same playbook.

## The 4 Twitter failure modes

### Failure 1 — Posting only when you have a "win"

Founders post a launch tweet, get 30 likes, post nothing for 3 weeks until the next "win." This is the worst pattern. You only show up for celebration → you're ignored when you actually need attention.

**Fix:** Post about the boring middle. The bug you fixed. The customer who churned. The decision you reversed. Process is more interesting than outcome.

### Failure 2 — Trying to go viral

Founders craft "perfect" tweets, edit them 10 times, never post. Or post bait that gets 200 likes from people who'll never buy.

**Fix:** Post first, edit later. Specificity beats virality. **A tweet with 50 likes from 50 people who fit your customer profile is worth 1,000 likes from random scrollers.**

### Failure 3 — Only tweeting (not replying)

The single biggest Twitter rookie mistake. Founders compose 20 tweets and never reply to anyone. Twitter is a *conversation* platform — replying is how you get seen.

**Fix:** 80% of your time on Twitter should be replies + engagement, 20% creation.

### Failure 4 — Posting on autopilot

Tools like Buffer + AI-generated tweets feel efficient but produce content nobody engages with. The whole point of Twitter is being a real human with a perspective.

**Fix:** Write your own tweets. Schedule them if you must (Tuesday–Thursday tend to perform), but write each one yourself. AI can help with phrasing — see Prompt It below — but the *idea* should be yours.

## Step 1 — Profile setup (30 minutes)

Twitter / X profiles get checked the moment someone interacts with your tweet. Three things matter:

### 1. Display name

Your real name + a tag for what you do.

Good: *"Shakir | Pexa Studio (21-day MVPs)"*
Bad: *"Shakir Mushtaq"*

The tag tells viewers in 1 second whether to follow you.

### 2. Bio

One sentence. Niche-of-niche. Same logic as LinkedIn (Chapter 17).

Good: *"Building SaaS MVPs in 21 days for non-technical founders | $3k–$6k fixed price | Posting daily what's working"*

Bad: *"Founder. Coffee enthusiast. Always shipping."*

### 3. Banner + pinned tweet

Banner: same logic as LinkedIn — clear tagline + URL.

**Pinned tweet:** Your single best, most representative tweet. Usually a thread that introduces your product or shares a specific story. The pinned tweet gets seen by every new follower.

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Help me write the perfect pinned tweet for [my product]. It should: (1) introduce who I am, (2) explain what I'm building, (3) include a specific recent metric, (4) include a CTA. Format as a thread of 4–6 tweets, each under 270 characters."*

## Step 2 — Find your "tribe"

Twitter is communities. Find yours.

### How to identify your Twitter tribe

For tech founders, the most active communities in 2026:
- **AI builders:** people building with Claude, GPT, Replicate, etc.
- **Indie hackers:** solo / small-team SaaS founders
- **Build-in-public:** broader bootstrapping community
- **YC alumni:** YC founders post and engage heavily
- **Specific tool/stack communities:** Next.js, Supabase, Cursor, Linear users
- **Niche industry tech:** legal tech, healthcare tech, fintech founders

### Workbook task — find 50 accounts to follow

In your tribe:
- Find 5 large accounts (50k+ followers) who post about your space
- Find 15 medium accounts (5k–50k) who post regularly
- Find 30 small/peer accounts (under 5k) who are at your stage

Follow all 50. These are now your daily Twitter rotation. Your home feed should be 80% your tribe, 20% news/general.

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"My niche is [specific niche]. Suggest 30 active Twitter / X accounts I should follow — mix of large (50k+ followers), medium (5k–50k), and peer accounts. Skip the obvious ones. Find people who post specifically about [my niche]."*

## Step 3 — The reply game

Pre-tweet, reply. **The fastest way to grow on Twitter is by being seen in someone else's replies.**

The mechanics: when you reply to a popular account's tweet, your reply is visible to their audience. If your reply is good — adds insight, disagrees thoughtfully, contributes data — viewers click on you and follow.

### The "good reply" formula

A good reply is 1–3 sentences that:
- Adds something the original poster didn't say
- Includes a specific number, story, or counter-point
- Reads like you actually engaged with the original (not generic)

Bad replies (don't do):
- *"This!"*
- *"Great thread!"*
- *"Following along"*
- Anything generic that could be on any tweet

Good replies:
- *"Tested this exact approach last week. We got [specific number]. The thing that surprised me was [insight]."*
- *"Disagree slightly. In my experience [counter-example]. Curious what made it work for you."*
- *"This matches what I'm seeing — though [variation] worked better for our [specific context]."*

### Workbook task — 10 replies per day

Pick 5 accounts from your "tribe" list. Reply to one tweet each, every day. **5 replies before you write a single tweet of your own.**

Then write your own tweets. By that point, your replies have already gotten you visibility, which means your tweets reach more people too.

## Step 4 — What to tweet

Six tweet types that work for build-in-public founders. Rotate them.

### Type 1 — Specific number tweets

*"Just hit $1,847 MRR. Up from $0 in 60 days. Channels that worked: [bullet list]"*

These are your viral candidates. Specific, vulnerable, useful.

### Type 2 — Process tweets

*"Decided today to kill our [feature] and replace it with [new approach]. Reasoning: [3 lines]"*

People love seeing decisions in real-time. Don't wait until the rebuild is done — tweet the reasoning when you're making the call.

### Type 3 — Question tweets

*"How are y'all handling [specific problem]? Mine isn't working — context: [paragraph]."*

Real questions get real engagement. **Don't ask questions you already know the answer to** — Twitter sniffs that out instantly. Ask things you genuinely don't know.

### Type 4 — Behind-the-scenes / screenshot

*"Here's what our dashboard looked like 60 days ago vs today. [screenshot]"*

Visuals beat text on Twitter. Combine a 1-line caption with a screenshot, graph, or product preview.

### Type 5 — Contrarian opinion

*"Unpopular: most SaaS founders are wasting their time on [common practice]. Here's why."*

Followed by 2–3 lines explaining your reasoning. **Make sure you actually believe it** — Twitter punishes fake hot takes by exposing them in replies.

### Type 6 — Threads (occasional)

A thread is a series of connected tweets. Use sparingly — once or twice a week max — for things that genuinely need more space than 280 characters. Long-form lessons, deep dives, breakdowns.

Thread format that works:
1. **Tweet 1:** the hook (compelling claim or stat)
2. **Tweet 2–10:** one point per tweet, with whitespace
3. **Last tweet:** CTA — follow for more / link to your product / DM for details

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Help me draft a Twitter thread on [topic]. Hook should grab attention in tweet 1. Body should be 6 tweets, each under 270 characters, each adding one specific point. Final tweet should have a CTA: [your CTA]."*

## Step 5 — Daily rhythm

The pattern that works:

- **Morning (15 min):** 5 replies on tribe accounts. Then 1 tweet (whichever type fits today).
- **Midday (5 min):** 1 quick reply or quote tweet
- **Evening (10 min):** Engage with replies on your morning tweet. Possibly one more tweet if you have something good.

That's ~30 minutes a day. **Held for 90 days = a real audience.**

Tools:
- **Tweet drafts:** native X drafts, or [Typefully](https://typefully.com) ($12/month)
- **Scheduling:** Typefully or Hypefury

## Step 6 — DM the right people

Same logic as LinkedIn (Ch 17). Within 48 hours of someone engaging meaningfully with your tweet (long reply, retweet with comment, multiple engagement), DM them.

Twitter DMs are casual — match the tone:

> *"Hey, saw you replied to my tweet on [topic]. Curious — what made you weigh in? You sound like you're dealing with the same thing."*

Open question. No link. No pitch. The DM is there to start a real conversation.

## Realistic numbers + timeline

For a tech founder posting daily:

- **Month 1:** 100–300 followers added, 30–500 impressions per tweet
- **Month 3:** 800–2,000 followers, 1,000–10,000 impressions per tweet, first inbound DMs about your product
- **Month 6:** 3,000–8,000 followers, 1–3 customers from Twitter
- **Month 12:** 10,000–30,000 followers if you're posting daily and engaging well, $5k–$50k MRR attributable to Twitter

The variance here is wider than LinkedIn because Twitter rewards specific niches more sharply. AI / dev tool founders go faster. Generic SaaS founders go slower.

## Real example — Cursor's Twitter strategy

The Cursor team — particularly Aman Sanger and Sualeh Asif — posted publicly almost every day for 2+ years.

The mix:
- 40% replies to other AI / dev accounts
- 30% product updates ("just shipped...")
- 20% hot takes about AI coding
- 10% threads breaking down customer wins

By 2024, Cursor was at $100M ARR. **Twitter was the single biggest channel** — most of their developers found Cursor through tech Twitter, not Google. The audience built over 2 years became the launch audience for every new feature, the recruitment pipeline for every hire, and the sales pipeline for every customer.

You can replicate the shape. Smaller scale. Same compounding.

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>Should I get verified / pay for X Premium?</b></summary>

For build-in-public, yes — $8/month. Verified accounts get reply boosting (your replies show up higher in conversations) which materially helps growth. The cost is trivial relative to the time investment in posting.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What about Twitter ads?</b></summary>

Skip them. Twitter / X ads have terrible ROI for early-stage SaaS. **Organic posting consistently outperforms paid by 5–10x for founders.** If you have ad budget, spend it on Reddit promoted posts (better targeting) or LinkedIn Sponsored Content (better intent).

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How do I deal with rage replies / negative engagement?</b></summary>

Don't. Block, mute, ignore. **Engaging with rage replies hurts your profile**: it pulls you off-niche, drains energy, and feeds the algorithm with conflict that won't bring real customers. The mute button is your friend. Use it liberally.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Should I start a Twitter / X thread for every blog post I publish?</b></summary>

Yes — repurpose every long-form piece into a 5–8 tweet thread. The thread is the hook; the link to the full piece comes at the end. **You'll get 10x more readers from the thread than from posting just the link.**

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What's the best time to post on Twitter / X?</b></summary>

For tech audiences globally: 8–10am ET (catches US morning + EU afternoon) and 2–4pm ET. For specific niches, watch when *your tribe accounts* are most active and post in those windows. Engagement matters more than timing — a great tweet at 11pm beats a mediocre one at 9am.

</details>
