# Chapter 20 — Snapchat & The Emerging Attention Channels

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/20-hero.png)

This is the chapter every founder skips. *"Snapchat? Really?"* Yes, really. And not just Snapchat — there's a layer of attention pools that non-technical founders entirely ignore, where teenagers and young adults spend 4+ hours a day, where the supply of business / SaaS content is essentially zero, and where the next generation of consumers is being formed.

You probably don't have a Snapchat account. Most founders don't. **That's exactly why the channel works.** Every founder Twitter, every founder LinkedIn, every founder TikTok is now competitive. Snapchat is wide open — and the audience is real, even if it's not the audience you initially imagined.

This is the closing chapter of the library, so it does double duty: a tactical guide to Snapchat + a strategic guide to identifying the *next* channel that emerges. Channels rotate. The skill of spotting one early is more durable than the playbook for any single channel.

---

> **Quick answer:** Snapchat Spotlight is the platform's TikTok-like vertical video feed — and it's the most underused short-form channel in 2026. It pays creators directly, has an active 16–34 demographic, and the supply of business content is near zero. Beyond Snapchat: keep watching for emerging channels that combine **(1) high attention time, (2) low founder competition, (3) algorithmic distribution to strangers, and (4) clear monetization or pipeline path.** When you spot a channel hitting all 4, be early. The founders who established themselves on TikTok in 2020 / LinkedIn in 2022 / Threads in 2023 / TikTok-of-2026 are 12 months ahead of the next wave.

## Why Snapchat specifically

Snapchat as a platform has 800M+ monthly active users in 2026. Significant for a platform many founders have written off. The user base:

- Heavily 16–34 demographic (matches consumer SaaS sweet spot)
- High daily watch time — users average 30–60 minutes/day
- Strong international presence (especially India, MENA, parts of Europe — where TikTok is sometimes restricted)

The product to focus on is **Snapchat Spotlight** — Snap's vertical-video feed, similar to TikTok / Reels / Shorts. Spotlight is algorithm-driven and pays creators directly via the Spotlight Reward Program (no follower minimum required to earn).

**Critical fact for founders:** Spotlight has very few accounts producing business / SaaS / how-to content. Almost all Spotlight creators are entertainment, lifestyle, comedy. The supply gap for "founder explaining what they're building" is even larger than on TikTok or Shorts.

## Should you actually be on Snapchat?

Honest filter:

### Yes if:

- Your customer is 16–34 (consumer apps, AI tools, dating apps, productivity for students/young pros)
- You have international audience priorities (especially India, MENA, parts of Europe)
- You're already producing short-form video (Ch 19) and the marginal cost to cross-post is near zero
- You want to be early on a channel that may become competitive in 18 months

### No if:

- Your customer is enterprise / corporate / >35 years old
- You have limited time and are not yet posting to TikTok / Shorts / Reels
- Your geography (US-only, B2B-only) makes Snapchat marginal

For most founders, Snapchat is a **tier-2 channel** — worth posting to *if* you're already producing short-form content (Chapter 19). It's not worth a separate content workflow.

## How Snapchat Spotlight is different

A few things to know if you're entering:

### 1. Posting interface

Spotlight uses Snapchat's own creation tools, which are clunky compared to TikTok. **Best practice:** create your video in CapCut, export, then upload to Spotlight. Don't try to record natively.

### 2. Tags matter more

Snapchat Spotlight relies more heavily on hashtags + "topics" than TikTok / Shorts. Choose 3–5 relevant tags per upload. Spotlight specifically promotes videos using trending topics tags.

### 3. The Reward Program

Snapchat pays Spotlight creators based on viewership (algorithm + engagement metrics). For high-performing accounts, this can mean $1,000+/month *paid by Snapchat itself*. Most other platforms (TikTok, Shorts) have similar programs but Snapchat's is one of the more generous in 2026.

This is a side benefit — your real goal is distribution, not Snapchat-payouts — but it offsets the cost of producing content.

### 4. Discovery via Search

Snapchat's search is increasingly important — and it's still relatively unoptimized vs TikTok's. Founders who hit relevant keywords in their captions and titles can dominate search-based discovery.

## Step 1 — Cross-post your TikToks

The simplest entry: take the videos you're already making for TikTok / Shorts (Chapter 19) and upload them to Snapchat Spotlight as well.

Process:
1. Export your video from CapCut (already in 9:16 vertical)
2. Open Snapchat → Spotlight → "+" to add new content
3. Upload your video
4. Add caption + 3–5 hashtags
5. Submit

Total time: ~3 minutes per video, in addition to your TikTok / Shorts workflow.

> **AI shortcut:** [Prompt It](https://promptit.app) → *"Generate 5 hashtags for a Snapchat Spotlight upload about [topic]. The video is for [audience]. Mix big-volume tags (#tech, #startup) with niche-specific tags. Avoid stale or banned tags."*

## Step 2 — Track what's working

Snapchat Insights (in your Spotlight account dashboard) tells you:
- Views per video
- Watch time
- Shares
- Earnings (if you're in the Reward Program)

Run the same audit you do for TikTok: which videos are popping? What do they have in common? Replicate.

## Step 3 — Native Snapchat features (optional advanced)

Once you've validated cross-posting works, you can experiment with Snapchat-native features:

### Snap Lenses (AR filters)

Branded AR lenses let users apply your filter to their snaps. Used well, this becomes viral on its own. Building a custom lens via [Lens Studio](https://lensstudio.snapchat.com/) is free.

This is genuinely a power-user move — most founders won't reach for it, and that's fine. But for B2C SaaS founders with a visual angle (filters, productivity, photography apps), AR lenses can drive real organic discovery.

### Snap Map

If you have a local business component (Pexa Studio's `/srinagar` page is an example — local Srinagar audience), Snap Map lets nearby users discover content geographically.

## Beyond Snapchat — the next emerging channels

Channels rotate. **Twitter peaked in 2014, LinkedIn went content-first in 2022, TikTok exploded in 2020, Threads tested 2023.** Each wave had founders who got there early and built lasting moats.

The next channel emergence will happen in 2026–2027. To spot it, watch for these 4 signals:

### Signal 1 — High attention time per user

Look for platforms where average user spends 60+ minutes/day. Anything below that is a niche, not a distribution channel.

### Signal 2 — Algorithmic distribution

The platform shows your content to *strangers* by default — not just your followers. Twitter (followers-first) lost ground to TikTok (algorithm-first) for this reason.

### Signal 3 — Low business content supply

Most platforms launch with entertainment content. The window where founders can establish themselves *before* the platform fills with business creators is the opportunity.

### Signal 4 — Clear pipeline path

Either direct monetization (Snapchat Reward, TikTok Creativity Program) OR strong outbound to your bio link / website.

When you find a platform hitting all 4, **be there early.** The first 1,000 followers on a new platform are 10x as valuable as the next 10,000 on a saturated one.

## Channels to watch in 2026–2027

A short list of platforms that *might* emerge as channel contenders. Don't bet your time on all of them — pick 1 to monitor:

### Threads (Meta)

Meta's Twitter alternative. Recovered from a flat 2024 to renewed growth in 2025–2026. Algorithm-driven feed. Lower business content supply than Twitter itself. **Worth watching.**

### Bluesky

Twitter alternative built on AT Protocol. Strong tech / dev audience as of 2026. If you're a dev tools founder, may be worth establishing presence.

### YouTube long-form (still underrated for founders)

Not new, but underused. A 10-minute YouTube video on "how I built X" can drive customers for years. The barrier is higher (production), but the durability is unmatched.

### Spotify / Apple Podcasts (podcast appearances)

If you're not a podcaster, *appear on existing podcasts.* Founders who guest on niche podcasts get warm-lead pipelines. Pitch 5 podcasts a month.

### Discord / Slack communities

Less "channels" than "rooms" — but the mods of large Discord servers and Slack groups can be your highest-leverage allies. Every active community of 5,000+ users is a potential distribution surface.

### Substack notes

Substack added a Twitter-like Notes feed in 2024. Still under-trafficked by founders. If you're already running a Substack newsletter (Ch 17), Notes is a free distribution layer.

## The 2-channel rule (forever)

You cannot maintain 6 distribution channels at once. **Pick 2 primary channels.** Everything else is secondary cross-posting.

For most founders:

- **B2B SaaS:** Primary channels = LinkedIn (Ch 17) + SEO (Ch 12–14). Secondary = Twitter (Ch 18), Reddit (Ch 16).
- **Indie / dev tool:** Primary = Twitter (Ch 18) + SEO (Ch 12–14). Secondary = Reddit (Ch 16), LinkedIn (Ch 17).
- **Consumer:** Primary = Short-form video (Ch 19) + SEO (Ch 12–14). Secondary = Reddit (Ch 16), Snapchat (this chapter).
- **Local / regional:** Primary = Reddit (Ch 16) + LinkedIn (Ch 17). Secondary = SEO (Ch 12–14), local short-form video.

Holding 2 channels at high quality for 12+ months beats spreading across 6 at low quality every time. **Discipline beats breadth at the founder stage.**

## Real example — Levels Health on YouTube

Levels (the metabolic health SaaS) is a useful case study in "pick a non-obvious channel and own it."

Levels' founder Sam Corcos went all-in on long-form podcast appearances and YouTube starting in 2020. While most health-tech founders chased Instagram and TikTok, Sam appeared on 50+ podcasts per year — many of them niche, technical, doctor-facing. Each appearance was an asset that compounded.

By 2024, Levels had ~$50M ARR. **Most of their early 10,000 customers came from Sam's podcast appearances and the long-tail YouTube content** — not from TikTok or LinkedIn.

The lesson: **the right channel is whichever one your customers consume + few competitors are using.** For Sam Corcos, that was podcasts. For Pieter Levels, it's Twitter. For Cursor, it's also Twitter. For your business, it might be something nobody on this list has thought of yet.

**Watch your specific audience. Find the channel they're on that nobody else is exploiting. Be there early.**

This is the last "Your Turn" of the library. Make it count.

1. **Audit your distribution today.** What channels are you currently active on? How many minutes/day do you spend on each? Is the time matched to where your customers actually are?

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>Is Snapchat really worth it for B2B?</b></summary>

For most B2B, no — the demographic gap is too wide. The exception: B2B products serving young professionals (e.g., productivity tools, AI tools, freelancer tools, learning platforms). For those, Snapchat's 18–24 audience is increasingly your future customer. Worth a 30-day cross-post test if you're already producing short-form video.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How do I know when to drop a channel?</b></summary>

Three signals: (1) you've posted consistently for 3+ months and have <500 followers, (2) channel-attributable revenue is $0 after 6 months, (3) the time-to-revenue ratio is dramatically worse than your other channels. If 2 of 3 are true, drop it. **Channels are real estate. Keep the ones that produce, sell the ones that don't.**

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Should I be on every emerging channel?</b></summary>

No. Pick one to monitor lightly. **Most emerging channels die.** The ones that don't will become obvious — their growth rate, founder presence, and pipeline impact will reveal themselves. Don't try to be early on 5 platforms; be early on 1.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What about WhatsApp / Telegram channels?</b></summary>

For local audiences (Pexa Studio's Srinagar segment is an example), WhatsApp and Telegram can be primary channels. They're highest-trust, lowest-noise distribution surfaces. Most modern founders ignore them because they don't scale to global audiences — but for hyper-local plays, they're underrated.

</details>
