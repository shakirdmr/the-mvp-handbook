# Chapter 1 — Finding the Right Idea

![Hero image — described in IMAGES_TODO.md](./assets/01-hero.png)

You probably already have an idea. The question this chapter answers is the only one that matters: **is it the right one?**

Most founders skip this question. They assume the idea they fell in love with three months ago is the idea they should spend the next two years of their life on. They build the thing, launch it, watch the launch flop, and then spend another six months convincing themselves the problem is "marketing." It almost never is.

The problem is almost always that they picked the wrong idea, in the wrong way, for the wrong reasons.

---

> **Quick answer:** A good MVP idea is one you'd build even if no one paid you. It comes from a problem you've actually felt, in a domain you actually understand, for users who actually exist in a market that's growing. If your idea fails any of those four tests, you're starting on bad ground — and the next 21 days of building won't save you.

---

## What "the right idea" actually means

Forget every brainstorm you've ever sat through. Forget the "how to come up with startup ideas" Medium article that told you to make a list. The right idea isn't a thing you *invent* — it's a thing you *notice*.

Paul Graham, in YC's Stanford CS183B Lecture #3, said it more clearly than anyone: **the way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself.**

The reason this works isn't poetic. It's practical. When you build for a problem you've felt, you have three unfair advantages:

1. **You don't need user research to know it's real.** You are the user.
2. **You know what doesn't work** because you've already tried the alternatives.
3. **You know which features matter** because you've felt the pain of them missing.

When you build for a problem you imagined, you have none of those. You're guessing on every decision. And founders who guess on every decision build products that miss every decision by a small amount, which compounds into a product that misses entirely.

Sam Altman put it bluntly in Lecture #1: *"It's much, much better to do something that a small number of users love than something that a large number of users like."* You can't make a small number of users love something if you don't even understand what they want.

## The four tests every idea needs to pass

You don't need a 40-page market analysis. You need four honest answers to four questions. If your idea fails any of these, **stop and rework it before you spend a single day building.**

### Test 1 — Have you actually felt this problem?

Not "have you observed it." Not "have you heard about it from a friend." Have *you* felt it. Have *you* been frustrated by it.

If your answer is no, you have two options:
- **Find someone who has,** and build with them as a co-founder or advisor.
- **Pick a different problem.** This isn't a small ask. The next 1,000 days of your life are about to revolve around this problem. Picking one you don't viscerally understand is choosing to be a tourist in your own company.

### Test 2 — Do other people have it?

Felt-it problems can still be too narrow. Maybe you're the only person who has it. Maybe ten other people have it, but they don't care enough to pay.

Quick gut check: Can you name **five real human beings**, by name, who have this problem? Not categories ("designers" or "small businesses") — actual people. If you can't, you're not close enough to the market yet.

If you can name three, you're closer than 80% of founders. If you can name ten, you're already further along than most YC founders on Day 1.

### Test 3 — Are they willing to pay?

This is where most ideas die. People will tell you "I'd love that!" all day long. People will not pay for things they "love" — they pay for things they need.

A useful filter: are these people *currently spending money* to solve this problem badly? Are they paying a freelancer? Are they paying for a tool that almost works? Are they paying with their time, doing the thing manually for hours every week?

**If money is already moving for this problem, you have a real opportunity.** If no money is moving, you have an interesting idea but a hard sell.

### Test 4 — Is the market growing?

You don't need a billion-dollar TAM. You don't need to be in AI just because AI is hot. But you do need to be standing on a sidewalk that's getting wider, not narrower.

A growing market forgives mistakes. A shrinking market punishes them. If you build the world's best fax machine startup today, no execution will save you.

Garry Tan, in his "Roll your way to a billion-dollar startup" essay, framed this perfectly: you don't need to start big. You just need to start in a place where rolling forward is possible.

## The three sources every good idea comes from

After tests, sources. There are basically three places founders get ideas that don't suck:

### Source 1 — Your job

The boring answer is also the best answer. The frustrations you've had at work, in your industry, in your professional context — these are gold. You've been a domain expert by accident.

This is where Slack came from (Stewart Butterfield's gaming team needed better internal chat). It's where Stripe came from (the Collison brothers found payments unbearably hard for developers). It's where most YC companies actually originate.

Your job has shown you 50 problems that nobody outside your industry knows exist. Pick one of those.

### Source 2 — Your hobby or community

If you spend 10 hours a week doing something — gaming, climbing, fly-fishing, modding cars, trading crypto, whatever — you know that community's pain points better than 99% of the world. That makes you a credible builder for them, which is the single most important asset early on.

Founders underestimate how much "credibility" is doing the heavy lifting before product-market fit. People want to buy from someone who is one of them.

### Source 3 — Your friends' frustrations (the second-best source)

If you don't have a job-related pain or a hobby-related pain, listen to your friends. The third time someone in your life complains about the same thing, write it down. The fifth time, call them and ask if they'd pay $50/month for a fix.

This is not as good as a problem you've felt yourself, but it beats inventing something at a whiteboard.

## The places good ideas don't come from

Equally important: a list of where founders go to get ideas that fail.

- **Reading "10 startup ideas you can build today" listicles.** Everyone reading those is now your competition.
- **Asking ChatGPT for ideas.** Same problem, multiplied by a million.
- **Brainstorming with a co-founder over beers.** This produces things you find clever, not things the market needs.
- **Cloning whatever's hot.** "Like X but for Y" rarely works unless you bring a real insight to the Y market.
- **Solving problems you've only read about.** Distance from the problem = guesses on every product decision.

If your idea came from any of these, that's not fatal — but you have to immediately do the work the founder who lived the problem already did. Go find users. Become the user. Move closer to the problem before you build.

## Real example — Airbnb's air mattress

Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia couldn't make rent in October 2007 in San Francisco. A design conference was in town. Hotels were booked solid. They didn't think *"there's a $500B accommodations market we should disrupt."* They thought *"we have three air mattresses and the rent is due."*

They put up a single page — `airbedandbreakfast.com` — for the conference. Three people booked. They charged $80 each. The first version of Airbnb was a scrappy hack to make rent.

What's interesting is what they noticed afterward. The travelers loved meeting locals. The locals loved the extra cash. Both groups asked them when the next event was. **The market told them what to build next, because they had built version one for a problem they'd actually felt.**

Compare that to the dozens of "Airbnb for X" startups that came after, almost all of which died. They were trying to repeat the result without repeating the process.

## Your Turn

Don't read on. Do the work. This chapter is useless if you skip it.

1. **Write down five problems you've personally felt in the last 90 days.** Be specific. Not "social media is broken." Try "I spend 45 minutes every Sunday assembling our family's grocery list across three apps."

2. **For each problem, score it 1–5 on three axes:**
   - **Frequency** — how often do I feel this? (1 = once, 5 = daily)
   - **Pain** — how much does it cost me in time, money, or sanity? (1 = mild, 5 = unbearable)
   - **Willingness to pay** — how much would I pay tomorrow to make it go away? (1 = $0, 5 = $50+/month)

3. **Pick the highest-scoring problem.** This is your candidate idea.

4. **Now do the friends test.** Send a 2-line message to five people you trust: *"Hey — quick question. Do you also struggle with [the problem]? How are you currently dealing with it?"*

5. **Read the answers, not the validations.** You're not looking for "yes, great idea!" You're looking for: do they spend time, money, or anger on this problem already?

If three out of five say "yes, and here's how I currently deal with it" — congratulations, you might have the right idea. Move to Chapter 2.

If three out of five say "huh, never thought about it" — drop this idea. Pick the next-highest-scoring problem. Repeat.

## FAQ

<details>
<summary><b>What if I have ten ideas? How do I pick?</b></summary>

Pick the one where you score highest on *frequency × pain × willingness-to-pay*. Then apply the four tests above. The one that survives all four is your candidate. If two survive, pick the one with the smaller, more specific user base — narrow markets are easier to validate.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What if my idea has no existing competition?</b></summary>

That's a yellow flag, not a green one. "No competition" usually means one of two things: (a) it's a genuinely new market and you're early, or (b) the market doesn't exist because nobody actually wants this. The way to tell which is to do the validation in Chapter 2 — talk to ten potential users. If they don't even understand what you're describing, you're in scenario (b).

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>What if I've never had a problem worth building for?</b></summary>

You have. Everyone has. You just haven't been listening for them. For the next two weeks, carry a notes app and write down every time you say "ugh" or "this is annoying." By the end of two weeks, you'll have 30 problems on the list. Five will be worth examining.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>Can I just build something I think is cool?</b></summary>

You can. Founders do this all the time. The hit rate is roughly 1 in 50. If you're okay with those odds and you have unlimited time and money, by all means. Most founders aren't, so this library assumes you want to ship something that has a real shot at making money in the next 60 days.

</details>

<details>
<summary><b>How do I know if my idea is too small or too big?</b></summary>

Too small: only 5 people on Earth want this, and they each want to pay $5/month. Too big: it requires regulatory approval, hardware, a 50-person team, or 18 months of R&D before launch. The right size for an MVP: a problem felt by ~10,000+ people that one or two people can build a useful version of in 21 days.

</details>
